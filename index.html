Understand Features of Static and Current Electricity

Description

A Literature MakerSpace activity that combines learning about the engineering design process and electrical circuits for digital storytelling. Students engage in a creative process - imagining alternate outcomes to narratives and retelling their own stories by incorporating sounds, lights, and movements created using components of their electrical circuits.

Standard 4.2: Analyze the Behavior of Current Electricity
  1. Draw and label the components of a complete electrical circuit that includes switches and loads (e.g., light bulb, bell, speaker, motor)
  2. Predict the effect of changing one or more of the components (e.g., battery, load, wires,) in an electric circuit
  3. Generalize the properties of materials that carry the flow of electricity using data by testing different materials
  4. Investigate materials that prevent the flow of electricity
  5. Make a working model of a complete circuit using a power source, switch, bell or light, and a conductor for a pathway

Language science students should use: Language science students should use: battery, complete circuit, incomplete circuit, current, conductor, insulator, pathway, power source, attract, compass, electromagnetism, magnetic force, magnetic field, natural magnet, permanent magnet, properties, repel, static electricity, temporary magnet, switch, load

Review: Electrical Circuits (10 minutes)

Note: List student responses on the board

Identifying Sensors, Actuators, and Conductors (20 minutes)

Materials: Powerpoint slide presentation; Snap Circuits Jr. 100 kit (1 per 4 students); 2 AA batteries per group (Distribute batteries after the demonstration)

Note: This activity builds on their knowledge of electrical circuits by introducing several different types of components

T: Today we are going to build on your knowledge of circuits by creating a circuit that will light up an LED light bulb. We will be doing this in a few different ways. How this will work today is that I will demonstrate what we will do, and then you will have the opportunity to work with your group to do the same thing. I need you to pay careful attention to the instructions as we go so as we can be careful with our circuit materials.

Note: Demonstrate how to create a basic circuit that lights up one LED bulb and explain how it works with each piece that is connected.

T: A conductor is an object or type of material that allows the flow of charge (electrical current) in one or more directions. Devices which perform an “Input” function are commonly called Sensors because they “sense” a physical change in some characteristic that changes in response to some excitation, for example heat or force and convert that into an electrical signal. Devices which perform an “Output” function are generally called Actuators and are used to control some external device, for example movement or sound.

T: (Show the electric light and switch example) When you close the slide switch (sensor), current flows from the batteries through the lamp and back to the battery through the switch. The closed switch completes the circuit – this is called a closed circuit. When the slide switch is opened, the current can no longer flow back to the battery, so the lamp goes out – also called an open circuit.

T: You can practice on your own by trying to assemble the circuit and lighting the lamp by pressing the slide switch.

Figure 1. Electric light and switch

S: Students practice assembling the circuit in their own group and lighting the lamp.

Notes: Elaborate further with additional examples of actuators.

T: When you close the slide switch (S1), current flows from the batteries (B1) through the motor (M1) making it rotate. Place the fan blade on the motor shaft and close the slide switch. DC motors are used in all the battery powered equipment requiring rotary motion, such as drills, electric toolbrush, and toy trains that run on batteries.

Figure 2. DC Motor and switch

S: Students practice assembling the circuit in their own group and spinning the motor.

T: When you close the slide switch (S1), the circuit (U2) should start sounding an up-down siren. Switch the sound on and off quickly and see if you can create even different effects.

Figure 3. Sound activated switch

S: Students practice assembling the circuit in their own group and listening to the alarm

T: Try adding and replacing the different types of sensors. Clap your hands close to the whistle chip (WC) or tab the base with your finger. Press and hold the press switch (S2) several times. Move your hand over the photoresistor (RP) and wave it.

Figure 4. Types of sensors

S: Students practice assembling the circuit in their own group and replacing sensors.

T: Some materials can carry the flow of electricity better than others. Try a paper clip for example, see if it can close the circuit. When you place a metal paper clip across the terminals, current flows from the batteries (B1) through the alarm, and back to the battery. The paper clip completes the circuit and current flows through the alarm. Place your fingers across the terminals and the LED does not light. Your body has too high a resistance to allow enough current to flow to light the LED. If the voltage (electrical pressure) would be higher, then current could be pushed through your fingers and the LED would light.

Figure 5. Conductive materials

S: Students practice assembling the circuit in their own group and replacing materials.

Read Aloud and Related Discussion (30 minutes)

Materials: 10 copies of This is Not My Hat by Jon Klassen; Graphic Organizer #1 and #2 for brainstorming; Lined paper for retelling the story

Students sit at the rug to listen to the story. Tables should be arranged for group work following the read-aloud and discussion.

T: (Show the book) Has anyone read this book before? Looking at the cover and reading the title, what do you think it will be about? Even if you have heard the story before, I want you to pay very close attention to the details in the text and the illustrations. We will be working with this story over the rest of this week.

Note: As you read aloud the story to the class, make sure to take a good deal of time to slowly show the illustrations to the class. You may want to remind them to pay close attention to the details in the illustrations.

After the story is completed,

T: What do you think happened at the end? Tell the person sitting next to you what you think happened there at the end of the story, and what evidence you have.

Call on individual students to share what they think happened and show textual/pictorial evidence that supports their conclusions. Next, ask the students four questions about the author’s decisions about style of text, illustrations, and design of the book.

T: “Why do you think Klassen decided to make the book in a horizontal format instead of a vertical format? How does this decision make the story different?”

(Focus on how this changes the pace of the story. You will be revisiting this concept with them later in the week.)

T: “What colors do you notice Klassen used? Why do you think he chose these colors? How does this decision affect the story?”

(Focus on how the color palette affects the mood of the story. You will be revisiting this concept with them later in the week.)

T: What do you notice about the way in which Klassen writes this story—his style? Does he use long sentences or short sentences or both? Is there a pattern to this? Does he repeat any phrases?

(Focus on how his style choices affects the tone of the story, building tension. You will be revisiting this concept with them later in the week.)

T: How does Klassen create humor in the story? Show us an example.

(Focus on how the pictures and the text contrast one another. Explain this is called “counterpoint” and is used in many picturebooks. You will be revisiting this concept with them later in the week.)

Statement of Challenge and Review of Circuit Components (10 minutes)

This discussion states the problem the students will be solving, and reviews student understanding of actuators and how these can integrate with the re-write they will be doing.

T: This is Klassen’s story. But what if we changed it? Your challenge over the next few days is to create something using SnapCircuits that will change the outcome of the story, and then retell your new version of the story.
Before getting into details, let’s think about this a little bit.
The outcome of the story, as you have presented with your textual evidence, is that the small fish was eaten and the big fish got his hat back.

(Some students may have argued that the small fish got away. You can state that as well as an outcome. Regardless, all should have agreed that the big fish did get his hat back at the end of the story.)

T: You have learned about how circuits have actuators to create sounds, light, or movement. Let’s talk about how those events could be used to change the story.

T: Imagine that the big fish was swimming along looking for a hat, he heard a sound or noise - How would this change the story? How would the outcome of the story been different?

T: Imagine that while the big fish was swimming along looking for his hat, he saw a light - How would this change the story? How would the outcome of the story been different?

T: Imagine that while the big fish was swimming along looking for his hat, he felt something move - How would this change the story? How would the outcome of the story been different?

(When students answer these questions, prompt them with follow up questions. Ask them about how the movement/light/sound affects the small fish and crab.)

This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer #2 (10-15 minutes)

T: You all know this story well. Now let’s turn to our challenge.

T: (Reads aloud and writes on the board) With your group, create something that would change the outcome of this story. Think about how you can use either light, sound, or movement to change the outcome of the story.

Pass out the This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer #2. Students just need one per group.

T: You are going to use this graphic organizer to plan out your change and the new outcome. Remember to answer each of the questions and fill in each box. Work together and use everyone’s ideas and input.
If you need help, raise your hand and one of the teachers will come to your group.

S: Using the This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer #2, students discuss and record how they could change the outcome of the story:

  • Write down the point(s) within the story where the lights would be used/occur.
  • Write down how the story would then differ, and thus cause a different outcome from the original after this point (tell the new version of the story starting from the point of change).
  • Fill out the plot diagram of their new story.

When helping students, use some of these questions to prompt student groups: What would the new outcome be? How would this happen? What creation is needed to change to the plot to achieve this new outcome? At what point in the story does the creation come in to play? Do characters use this new device? Does a new setting occur? Does the changed outcome affect the mood and theme of the original setting?

More Work on This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer #2 (15 minutes)

Materials: This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer #2 from yesterday, Photo images on slides of examples, 10 copies of This is Not My Hat, 10 SnapCircuit Sets, 10 Boxes, construction paper, paint, glue, scissors, staplers, straws or popsicle sticks, assorted craft supplies

T: Yesterday we read This is Not My Hat, mapped out the story, and thought about how we might change the outcome of the story by using light, sound, or movement.

Today you are going to finish your This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming graphic organizer and decide upon how your group is going to meet the challenge of changing the outcome of the story and retelling the story.

Here’s that challenge exactly:

  1. Using snap circuits, create something involving light, sound, or movement that would change the outcome of the story.
  2. Create a video of this new retelling of the story.

T: Now, remember, the circuit you create to change the outcome of the story has to make sense within the story.
For example, if you are going to use a light to change the outcome, you can’t just have a light appear out of nowhere in the ocean. A light would have to come from a lighthouse, or from a deep-sea fish that has a natural light, or from a scuba diver holding a flashlight, or a search light on a boat, or maybe the light from an alien ship, etc. This book is a fantasy (because fish don’t really talk and don’t really wear hats), but it is still grounded in some level of reality.
A movement in the water has to come from some source- another fish, a whirlpool, a boat, etc. Because this book is a fantasy (fish don’t really talk and don’t really wear hats), but it is still grounded in some level of reality.

T: So, by the end of today, your group will have decided the device you are going to use and begun creating the materials for retelling the story with the new outcome.
Use the next 15 minutes to finish your This Is Not My Hat Brainstorming Graphic Organizer. If you have questions, raise your hand and one of the teachers will come to your group. Feel free to use the booklets in Snap Circuit sets for ideas.

Let students work for 15 minutes and circulate the room helping if needed. If students are finishing, encourage them to go ahead and build the device with their Snap Circuits. As you circulate, remind them of the need of integrating the device into the story in a reality-based way.

Discussion of Author Style and Work on Retelling Integration (20 minutes)

T: Today we are going to revisit the story and discuss the way the author uses language in the story. Then you will spend some time working with your same group on your retelling of the story using some of the same techniques the author uses.
You will use the retelling script you create when you videotape your new version of the story tomorrow. Tomorrow you are going to create a video of your version of the story that uses your device and has a different story outcome/conclusion.

T: What do you think style means? (Students respond.) What do you think author’s style means?

*Show slides for author’s style while discussing them with students

Author’s style is the way the author uses words and constructs sentences to tell a story.
Sometimes authors like to use really long sentences.

NFor example, this sentence is from Because of Winn-Dixie by Kate DiCamillo. She is talking about the dog, Winn-Dixie
“And then he crept up on the couch with us in this funny way he has, where he gets on the couch an inch at a time, kind of sliding himself onto it, looking off in a different direction, like it’s all happening by accident, like he doesn’t intend to get on the couch, but all of a sudden, there he is.”

Sometimes authors like to use very short sentences.
For example, this sentence is from You Go First by Erin Entrada Kelly. She is talking about a family conversation.
“Things were definitely weird.”

Sometimes authors use lots of descriptive words to create vivid images for their readers to see in their minds.
For example, this sentence is from The Epic Fail of Arturo Zamora by Pablo Cartaya. He is describing a manager in a kitchen restaurant.
“Martin was in an especially bad mood today and I could hear him yelling orders at the cooks. He waddled around like a hippo standing on two feet, and the mixture of highly perfumed deodorant and fried grease was making me queasy.”

And sometimes authors don’t give as many details so readers can imagine the story on their own.
For example, this sentence is from Dazzle Ships: World War I and the Art of Confusion by Chris Barton.
“Wilkinson’s responsibilities included patrolling the waters off southwestern England and sweeping for mines. He saw a lot of ships as part of his job.”

I am going to reread parts of This Is Not My Hat. I want you to pay attention to the words and sentences that Jon Klassen uses to tell the story. We are going to think about some of things we discussed the first day we read the story.
Begin rereading This Is Not My Hat.
Pause after page opening (spread) 2 and ask: “What do you notice about the sentences the author is using so far?” “Are they long or short?”

  • Short – 4 short sentences

Pause after page opening (spread) 6 and ask: “What do you notice about the sentences in these pages? Are there any patterns that you notice? Are any words used over and over again? Which ones?”

  • Long phrases, “and even,” “probably won’t”

Pause after page opening (spread) 9 and say: “Did you notice how the pace of the story just became faster? Klassen put several sentences together in clumps so that the person reading aloud reads faster. Then he slowed the reader down on the next page by moving to one sentence that is on the farther opening. This is an example of changing pace and adding dramatic emphasis. We talked about that when we read the story on Tuesday.

When you do your retelling, you will want to use pacing like Klassen with short and then long sentences in order to build dramatic tension.

Let’s also look closely at this image on the spread. (Show page opening 9 again. Point to the picture of the big fish and the crab, and then to the words “So I am not worried about that.”)

Part of the reason why the pace slowed down here was so the reader would notice the contrast on this page. The reader notices the counterpoint—the little fish is saying there is no reason to be worried, but the image in the illustration is telling the reader that he SHOULD be worried. The words and the images are not the same.

When you do your retelling, you will want to use counterpoint like Klassen where sometimes the images will not match your words.

Read on to page opening (spread) 11. Pause after reading this page.

Most of the time Klassen does not use vivid description in his writing because this is a picturebook and he can show the details in the illustrations. When he does use vivid description, it’s usually a reminder about something happening in the story.

Here he writes, “Where the plants are big and tall and close together.” He does this to emphasize that the little fish should be safe here. But it’s actually a hint to the reader that the little fish is not likely going to be safe.

When you do your retelling, you will want to use vivid details when you want to emphasize something in the story.

Retelling Work: Scripts (20 minutes)

T: Now you are going to work on your retelling. For the next 20 minutes, use your Graphic Organizer from yesterday and the hints from today about author style to write the script for your retelling.

Your script should have the original pages of the book and then the part you are redoing. It must incorporate your device in a way that makes sense and changes the outcome of the story. It also must be in the same style as Klassen’s style.

Use the storyboard and hints worksheet. It encourages you to think about the video image you might have showing while you are reading that page aloud.

Students return to tables and work in groups. Teacher circulates room and provides assistance where needed.

Literature MakerSpace Activity (20 minutes)

T: For the last 20 minutes of our time, you should finish your script, and also continue working on the scenery for your box. Tomorrow we will be finishing up the scenery, practicing the retelling, and then videotaping it.
